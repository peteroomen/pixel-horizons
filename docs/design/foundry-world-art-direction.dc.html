WORLD ART DIRECTION
Pixel Horizons · the look inside the FOUNDRY frame
The HUD (FOUNDRY) is done and good — this pass is everything behind the glass: the ship, the Bloom, and the infested lane that are currently programmer art. One thesis runs through all of it: two palettes, one law. The world is painted in Resurrect 64 — muted, ramped, shaded. The UI speaks in FOUNDRY's five pure accents. The eye never confuses them because ramped-and-muted = lore you look at; flat-and-pure = a signal you can act on. Everything below is buildable by a solo dev from generated + recolored + simple hand-drawn pixels. All art on this page is live <canvas>, integer-scaled, nearest-neighbor — exactly your render path.
1 · TWO PALETTES, ONE LAW
RESURRECT 64 — the world
Governs every sprite, planet and backdrop. Always used in 3–5 step ramps and never at full saturation. Organised here exactly as Kerrie Lake's ramps ship — I've labelled which ramps each faction draws from.
Collective — slate-greens, steel violets, teals & blues (cool, hard, machined).
Bloom — purples, magentas/rose, sick yellow-greens (warm, wet, bio).
Shared void — the darkest violets bottom out into FOUNDRY's #0b0d18.
FOUNDRY — the UI & the signals
Five pure accents. They live in the HUD — and they're the only fully-saturated pixels allowed to touch the world, reserved for gameplay-critical FX. Each one has a Resurrect 64 "cousin" the world art ramps toward, so the two palettes rhyme instead of clash.
ORANGE
action. World use: muzzle flash, your projectiles, thruster ignition. Cousin ramp fb6b1d f9c22b
RED
enemy / danger. World use: incoming-fire flash, hit shake. Cousin ea4f36 e83b3b

GREEN

THE BRIDGE
RULE
If a pixel is muted and part of a ramp, it's the world — lore, mass, atmosphere. If a pixel is flat and pure-saturated, it's a signal — a thing the player can read or act on. The world art ramps toward the accent it's near (your engine glows toward cyan; spores glow toward green), so the frame reads as one game — but a ramp never actually reaches the pure accent. That last step belongs to the HUD.
2 · MECHANICAL vs ORGANIC — THE LAW
The fiction is a hard contrast, so the pixels must be too. Every asset obeys one of two grammars. When in doubt: the Collective is drawn with a ruler; the Bloom is drawn with a sponge, and it deliberately breaks the grid the Collective respects.
COLLECTIVE
mechanical · drawn with a ruler
SHAPE  Rectilinear. 90° & 45° only, bilateral symmetry. Reads as assembled from boxes.
EDGE   1px selective outline in a darker hull tone. Crisp. Panel-line seams.
SHADE Hard cel banding, light from top. 3-step ramp per material + a cyan rim where energy runs.
DITHER Ordered / checkerboard, grid-aligned, sparingly — to fake a 4th metal tone.
MOTION Rigid & snappy. On-grid moves, 1–2px recoil kick, 2-frame thruster flicker. No squash.
THE BLOOM
organic · drawn with a sponge
SHAPE  Bulbous, asymmetric, branching. No straight run >4px. Overhangs & stray pixels break the grid.
EDGE   Thick wet selout (violet), with a brighter pink "wet rim" highlight. Looks slimy.
SHADE Blotchy, not banded. Subsurface sick-green glow in pockets. Brighter flesh veins.
DITHER Noise / random mottle. Anti-grid — it's how flesh reads as wet and uneven.
MOTION Breathing. Sub-pixel wobble, slow squash-stretch, throbbing organs, swaying tendrils. Never still.
Left: ordered dither — the Collective's way to stretch a 3-tone ramp into 4 without a new colour, every pixel on the grid. Right: noise dither — the Bloom's mottle, seeded-random, deliberately ignoring the grid. Same two source colours; opposite grammar. This single contrast is most of the readability.
3 · THE GUNSHIP — VISIBLE MODULE COMPOSITING
Core fantasy: what you installed is what you see, and it's also your deck. So the ship is never one sprite — it's a base hull silhouette plus per-module overlay sprites composited onto fixed mount slots. Same data drives the card you hold and the part bolted to your hull. 4 · THE BLOOM — GRUNT & BOSS ORGANS
The Bloom is wet coral-fungus that breaks the Collective's clean grid. Its silhouette sheds stray pixels; its body throbs. The boss problem is the interesting one: its targetable organs must read as distinct, attackable growths fused to the body — not HUD bars. Solution via the bridge rule: an organ is organic flesh that has grown a flat pure-accent core. That core is the only non-ramped pixel on the creature, so the eye instantly tags it "I can hit this," while the flesh around it stays lore.
MAWLING · grunt
Asymmetric biter. One sick-green eye, a wet maw, shed spores. No straight edge anywhere.
SPORE-SAC
ARMOR-NODE
ANCHORMAW · lane boss
SPORE-SAC — a soft translucent bladder, throbbing. Flat green core = targetable. Kill it to stop the infestation cards it injects into your hand.
ARMOR-NODE — a hardened amber-plated nodule grown over the flesh. Reads "shelled." Kill it to strip the boss's ARMOR. Its plates are the only rectilinear thing on the creature — co-opted Collective wreckage.
Both are fused to the mass with continuous flesh and a shared wet rim — distinct silhouette, same body. Coral roots anchor it to the lane (it's part of the infestation, not a free ship).
Solo-dev path: blob silhouette + noise-dither flesh is forgiving to draw — no clean lines to get wrong. Organs are small reusable sub-sprites stamped onto any boss body, so new bosses = new silhouette + a pick of organs. The targeting reticle is HUD (FOUNDRY), drawn by the UI layer, not painted into the sprite.
5 · THE HYPERSPACE LANE — INFESTED, NOT EMPTY
Right now the lane is a flat starfield with one diagonal streak. But the Bloom infests the lanes — so the backdrop should feel like you're swimming down an infected throat between safe nodes, not floating in open space. Layered for parallax: deep void → a distant cool realspace beacon (where you're trying to get) → the lane's diagonal current → Bloom coral creeping in from the top & bottom edges → drifting spore motes. Procedural-friendly: Deep-Fold generates the starfield/nebula; the coral is stamped blob clusters; motes are particles.
Depth by recolor. Far coral is dimmed toward void and shifted cooler; near coral is brighter and warmer. One blob sprite, two tints — instant parallax with no extra art.
The beacon gives direction. The one cool cluster is the realspace node you're traveling to (ties to TRAVEL 3/9 in the HUD). It's the only cool light in a warm-infected field, so the eye always knows "out is that way."
It lives. In-engine the coral breathes, motes drift across the current, and nodes pulse green on a slow sine — all cheap per-frame transforms, no new frames to draw.
6 · HOW THE HUD FRAMES THE WORLD
Full-bleed, plates float. The world canvas runs edge-to-edge under the FOUNDRY plates — no boxed viewport. The plates are instruments bolted over the glass, exactly as the HUD spec already floats them.
Edge vignette. A soft darkening at the screen edges sinks the world into FOUNDRY's void so the plates sit on darkness, never on a busy starfield. Costs nothing, buys legibility.
Scanlines on the world only. A 1px CRT scanline + faint flicker lives on the world layer — it's "the signal on the tube." The HUD plates stay razor-sharp DOM: the glass you read through is clean.
The boundary. A 1px steel hairline where a plate meets canvas — the mount line. Chunky world pixels vs crisp small HUD pixel-font is itself the console-over-viewport cue.

7 · BUILDABLE BY ONE PERSON
PLANETS & SPACE
Deep-Fold generators, then quantised to Resurrect 64 and dimmed into the void. Zero hand-pixeling — recolor is the whole job.
SHIPS
A few base-hull silhouettes + a reusable module-overlay library, composited at runtime. The deck literally draws the sprite. New builds cost no new art.
THE BLOOM
Blob + noise-dither flesh is the forgiving end of pixel art — no clean lines to botch. Organs & tendrils are stamped sub-sprites shared across every Bloom enemy.
FX & SIGNALS
The pure FOUNDRY accents — muzzle, shield bubble, hit-flash, organ cores — are flat shapes drawn by code, not painted frames. The bridge rule is the FX budget.
Net new hand-drawn art to ship this language on the combat screen: ~1 base hull, ~6 module overlays, ~3 Bloom bodies, ~4 organ/tendril stamps. Everything else is generated, recolored, composited, or coded. That's the point — the look survives having no dedicated artist.

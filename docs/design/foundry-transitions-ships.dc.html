THE TRANSITION FX LAW — FIVE ACCENTS, FIVE BEATS
A transition is where the world is in motion, so the bridge rule does its loudest work: the muted Resurrect-64 scene carries the place, and a pure FOUNDRY accent fires only on the beat you're meant to feel — the instant of drop, ignition, contact. Reuse exactly these accents and nothing new; the player already reads them from combat.
ORANGE · IGNITION
pod thruster burn, descent retro-fire, surface dust kick.
CYAN · DROP-OUT
the realspace flash, ship engine wash, atmosphere skin.
AMBER · RE-ENTRY
the pod's heat trail, hazard chevrons, the atmosphere burn.
RED · IMPACT
touchdown shake flash — one frame, the moment of contact.
GREEN · THE BLOOM
spore-glow on the lane walls you tear out of — it lives here.
1 · LANE DROP — HYPERSPACE → ORBIT (ARRIVAL)
You're swimming down the infested lane; you reach a planet node and punch out into realspace orbit. The fiction: the Bloom-grown tunnel is rushing past, it decelerates, a cyan drop-flash, and the walls collapse behind you as the planet swings into view ahead. Plays on every planet arrival — 1.5–3s, must survive 375px. Below: the live loop, then the keyframe breakdown with timing.
LIVE — LOOPING
full-bleed, plates float over it · ~2.4s + a beat of orbit hold
KEYFRAME STORYBOARD
0.0–1.2s · TRAVEL
coral tunnel rushing, speed-lines, node ahead.
1.2–1.7s · DECEL
streaks shorten, ship eases back, tunnel slows.
1.7–1.9s · DROP-FLASH
cyan bloom fills frame — the punch-out beat.
1.9–2.3s · COLLAPSE
tunnel rushes away behind; planet swings in.
2.3s+ · ORBIT
calm realspace, ship floats, planet fills lower frame.
Camera. Static frame; the world moves. Tunnel scrolls toward camera (z-rush faked by speed-lines lengthening + coral walls scaling out), then settles. Ship never leaves screen-center — only a 2px recoil ease on decel. Ends framed with the planet occupying the lower third.
Build cost. Tunnel walls = the §5 lane coral, two scaling sprite-strips (near/far) — existing art. Speed-lines + spore motes = particles. Drop-flash = one full-screen cyan quad fading on a 0.2s curve (shader/CSS tween). Planet = one Deep-Fold sprite slid up on an ease-out. No new frames to hand-draw.
Feel / mobile. The deceleration is the satisfying part — ease-out on the scroll speed sells "arrived." At 375px it's identical: the flash and planet read even when tiny; speed-lines just thin to 1px. Total ≤2.4s keeps re-entries snappy on repeat.
2 · LANE LAUNCH — ORBIT → HYPERSPACE
The counterpart to the Lane Drop: you finish at a node and punch back into the lane. The fiction runs in reverse — ship hangs in calm orbit, the drive spools up (engine wash swells, the planet drifts away beneath you), a cyan jump-flash, and the infested tunnel blooms inward and rushes past as you accelerate into the Bloom's throat. Plays on every departure — same 1.5–3s budget, mirrors the arrival so the pair feels like one breath in / one breath out.
LIVE — LOOPING
orbit hold → spool-up → jump-flash → the lane swallows you
KEYFRAME STORYBOARD
0.0–0.9s · ORBIT
calm realspace, ship floats over the planet.
0.9–1.5s · SPOOL-UP
engine wash swells, planet drifts away.
1.5–1.7s · JUMP-FLASH
cyan bloom — the punch-in beat.
1.7–2.5s · ACCELERATE
coral funnel blooms inward, streaks build.
2.5s+ · CRUISE
full lane travel — hands off to the map.
It's the Drop, reversed. Exact same assets and curve, run backwards: speed eases up instead of down, the funnel grows from nothing instead of collapsing, the planet slides out the bottom instead of in. Build one timeline, play it forward for launch and reversed for arrival — half the work.
The new beat is the spool-up. Before the flash, the orange + cyan engine wash swells behind the ship for ~0.6s — the wind-up that makes the jump feel earned. That glow is a scaling additive quad, not new art.
Feel / mobile. The acceleration into the tunnel is the satisfying inverse of the arrival's deceleration. Identical at 375px: the planet exits down-frame, the funnel forms center, the flash carries the cut. ≤2.5s so departures never drag on repeat.
3 · DROP POD DEPLOY — ORBIT → SURFACE
From orbit you fire the clone down in a pod. One continuous fall, two framings: Scene A holds on the ship as the pod detaches and shrinks toward the planet; at atmosphere-contact the camera rides the pod down and the view becomes the side-scroll surface for Scene B — streak in, retro-burn, impact, door opens, go-time. The cut is hidden inside the heat-glow. 2–4s total; the return trip is a plain fade (not shown).

A → B in one move · the atmosphere-glow masks the scene swap
KEYFRAME STORYBOARD — A: ORBITAL  ·  B: SURFACE

ship in orbit, planet below, pod docked.

One move, not two scenes. Treat it as a single camera that hands off: A is locked to the ship, the pod is a sprite shrinking on a path toward the planet; when its heat-glow peaks it fills frame and B fades up underneath at full scale. The player never sees a hard cut — the amber burn is the wipe.
Build cost. Pod = one 12×16 sprite (the §2 overworld pod, reused) at 3 scales. Heat trail + dust = particles. Impact = 1-frame red flash + 3px camera shake (code, not art). Door-open = 2 pod frames you already have. Planet = the same orbital sprite from Lane Drop. Everything composites or recolors.
Minimal version / return. If budget's tight, skip the camera ride: hold Scene A to atmosphere, hard-flash to B mid-streak — still reads. The return trip (pod → orbit at run's end) is just a reverse-fade with the launch-burn from the overworld pod — no bespoke animation needed.
4 · SHIP REFINEMENT — HULL FIRST, MODULES AS DETAIL
The current Gunship reads as a cluster of boxes — modules nearly as big as the hull. The fix keeps the compositing system (what you install is what you see) but rebalances the ratio: a dominant 64×32 hull silhouette that reads first, with modules shrunk to 8×8 / 12×8 detail parts that bolt onto small mount points. The hull is the ship; the modules are what it's carrying.
BEFORE · modules overwhelm
Big blocks bolted on — silhouette is a pile of crates. You can't tell the hull from the cargo.
AFTER · hull reads first
One clear vessel; small distinct components perched on mounts. Same 5 modules — a quarter the visual weight.
MODULE GRAMMAR — DISTINCT BY TYPE, ~8×8 / 12×8
WEAPON
barrel / turret — points forward
ENGINE
thruster bell — cyan when lit
UTILITY
dish / scanner antenna
EMPTY SLOT
open mounting bracket
Mount logic: the hull carries fixed mount points — weapon mounts ride the dorsal spine & one belly hardpoint, the engine mount sits in the rear bell housing, utility on the forward deck. An empty slot shows the bare bracket, so a half-built ship still reads as intentional, not broken. The muzzle / engine wash stay the only pure-accent pixels — orange when a weapon fires, cyan when the drive's lit.

6 · NEW BLOOM SHIPS — CRUISER & SWARM
The existing Bloom (Mawling, Anchormaw) are blobby masses — samey. Two new silhouettes that break that, both still wet-organic (drawn with the sponge), both using the Bloom ramp with a distinct accent so they don't read as the same creature twice: a structured Cruiser and a distributed Swarm.
BLOOM CRUISER · structured
A coral skeleton grown into a warship's shape — an exposed calcified rib-spine carries sacs of flesh slung between the bones. Big, deliberate, architectural. The rib-white structure is the new read; rose-magenta flesh, a violet wet rim. Threatening because it looks engineered by instinct.
BLOOM SWARM · distributed
Not one body — a loose shoal of a dozen spore-pods that move as one, bound by faint mycelial threads. Each pod a sick-green mote with a single eye; the cloud has a silhouette but no center. Threatening because there's nothing single to kill — hit it and it re-forms around the gap.
Why they read apart at small scale: the Cruiser is dense and vertical with a hard pale rib-structure (rare straight-ish lines on a Bloom thing — co-opted growth); the Swarm is sparse, wide and gapped, all negative space. One is architecture, one is a cloud — opposite silhouette logic, same wet grammar & ramp. Build: Cruiser = blob body + a stamped rib-overlay; Swarm = one pod sub-sprite scattered on a noise field with per-pod sine drift — both cheap, both reuse the shared organ/eye stamps.
